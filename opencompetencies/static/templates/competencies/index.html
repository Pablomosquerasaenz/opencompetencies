Open Competencies (alpha)

Open Competencies gives educators control over education standards. It allows us all to openly create, share, and revise the standards we want our students to meet.

The software development world is about 40 years ahead of the education world in terms of sharing open tools and resources to help everyone focus on the interesting and challengin parts of their work. Open Competencies aims to bring some of these ideas to education:

How Open Competencies helps:

Most education standards are written by an external body.

Open Competencies makes it easy to develop your own set of standards, by using an existing structure to organize the standards you are developing. more

Most education standards are not subject to continuous revision.

When you see a way to improve your school's standards, you are free to revise them at any time, and it is easy to do so.

If you like a system that another school is using, it is often very difficult to help your own school adopt that school's system.

If you like another school's standards on Open Competencies, you can "fork" that school's system. This lets you start working with your own copy of any school's entire system in a minute or two.

Most schools have one set of "graduation requirements", ignoring the fact that students have many different goals in life.

Open Competencies allows you to create "Pathways" through a school's competency system. These are sets of standards that a student identifies as relevant and meaningful, which they must meet in order to graduate from high school.

Many technology-focused educational resources are built more with a profit motive in mind than with a goal of serving all students.

Open Competencies is a completely open project, including source code and content.

Problem: Most education standards are written by an external body.

Most education standards are written by a group of people who are pulled together for the specific purpose of writing a set of standards. This might be a number of people from around a state education system who are pulled together throughout a school year to develop an individual state's standards, or it might be a group that is sponsored full-time to develop a set of standards.

The main problem with this model is that the sponsoring group almost always retains copyright over the standards that are developed. Using these standards commits you to going wherever this sponsoring body leads.

How Open Competencies helps:

Open Competencies will make it easy to develop your own set of education standards, by giving you a dedicated tool for creating standards. Right now you can start from scratch, or you can start with a copy of another school's system. Soon you will be able to start from scratch, and bring in any group of standards from any other school to create your own mix of standards that suit your students' needs.

Try it out:

This is a bare-bones demonstration of what I'd like Open Competencies to be. Please kick the tires a bit, and share some feedback if you'd like to see this project fully developed.

You can start by looking at some sample competencies from Sample High School. Then go back and create your own school; once you create your own school, you will be able to fork an existing school's system. This forking behavior is one of the core features of the Open Competencies project.

You can also check out some sample pathways. A pathway is a collection of related elements of the competency system. You can check out a pathway that represents minimum graduation requirements for high school students, and a pathway that showing what a student who wants to become a physicist should focus on. This is another main feature of Open Competencies: the ability to identify different graduation requirements for individual students, based on their post-high school plans.

If you are a developer, you can check out the code from github. You can share feedback there as well.
